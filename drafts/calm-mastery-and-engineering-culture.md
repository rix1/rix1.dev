_The Bear_ made the chaos so real in certain episodes that I had to take a break
and go back to it later. I loved the season nevertheless. But there are two
scenes that were just peace.

The first is when the seasoned chef speaks with Carmy about tying the roast
chicken. It is quiet. The conversation is about food and cooking. He seems kind,
at ease, happy, and still in control of his expertise while talking about the
act of making something.

He teaches while putting Carmy at ease. He almost makes it seem possible to be
sane and really good. That kind of calm focus feels non-negotiable if you want
to make good things.

The chef in the scene is Thomas Keller, one of the most decorated American
chefs. For my 30th birthday, I was fortunate enough to get a MasterClass pass. I
tried watching a bunch of classes, but no one really clicked for me. One day I
stumbled upon Keller's classes. I had no idea who he was, but I got pulled in
and ended up spending a weekend binging them.

Since then, I have regularly rewatched those classes and renewed my subscription
every year just to avoid losing access. When he appeared in _The Bear_, I smiled
the whole way through, thinking about all the different things he had taught me.

## What can we learn from Keller?

Keller talks about six rituals:

1. Organization
2. Efficiency
3. Critical feedback
4. Repetition
5. Rituals
6. Teamwork

The reason Feriekolonien is so much fun is that I get to practice all six
rituals on a daily basis.

## Leaders and followers

A leader is someone others follow. If you want to accomplish great things, and
you cannot do that alone, you need to make it easy for people to follow you.

Keller illustrates one way of doing this by setting up a template:

> This is where the soup bowls go. This is where the cutlery goes.

Here are a few templates I think an engineering leader can provide:

> This is how we do PRs. This is how we organize the code and application. These
> are the tools that integrate with your editor. These are the automated
> workflows we run on CI. This is how I gather input. This is how I demo my work.

So what makes a good template?

The best templates do not even have to be communicated verbally. They are built
into the context you are operating in, or their affordance communicates that
they are a template.

## Explicit and implicit templates

I think of explicit templates as the default choice: built-in, a pit of success,
where people fall into doing the right thing. A lot of design theory can be
applied when creating these templates.

Example from the kitchen: You see cutlery being stacked in a bowl. If you have a
dirty spoon in your hand and are looking for a place to put it, placing it
together with the others should feel natural. Similarity and proximity do the
work.

Example from engineering: formatters, linters, application frameworks, and CI
workflows. This is part of the reason we use Django and Next.js. Others have
created templates for us that reduce the number of small decisions we have to
make every day.

This is why it is important to consider your needs when evaluating technology:
will this reduce the number of decisions we have to make on a daily basis?

## Calm mastery

If the path to greatness for any craftsperson is filled with external
obstacles, then the person showing you the ropes should actively minimize the
friction they create while teaching.

If they want to make excellent things, and they know they cannot do it alone,
this is the only justifiable behavior.

Calm focus is not a nice-to-have. It is part of the craft.

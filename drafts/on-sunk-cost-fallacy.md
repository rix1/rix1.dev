Someone once asked:

> Does anyone else spend a lot of time doing something a specific way and then
> find a way better method? What happens in that case? Do you implement the way
> better method or just stick to the original?

What follows are my ramblings about work and sunk cost fallacy.

There is valuable learning in every process. Working with code already means
working in a highly optimized way compared to manual labor. But it is useful to
get experience and see both sides of this, not only because design tools let you
iterate a lot faster than code, but because there is so much valuable learning
in the iterations.

Last week my girlfriend and I sat down to create a yearly calendar that was sold
out. We wanted to copy the design, so we got started in Illustrator. The fun,
but also annoying, thing about exercises like this is that you always discover
smarter ways of doing things along the way.

I usually bookmark these discoveries mentally and say: let's continue for now,
but next time I have to do this, I am definitely doing it the smarter way.

But it is a tradeoff. Some tasks are not worth optimizing. I also take pride in
being mindful of the sunk cost fallacy.

There are many things to take into consideration.

If you are not working alone, your suboptimal solution might eventually be copied
and reused by someone with less experience than you. In a small way, this might
hinder their development because you were either lazy or too proud to change the
original.

## Process

When I discover better ways of doing something, I usually make a mental bookmark
of the new technique and try to test it out either in a controlled environment
or the next time I do that thing. For the ongoing iteration, I am most likely to
continue doing it the same way I had previously done it.

But it depends on a bunch of factors, mostly related to the fact that I am, by
definition, not experienced with doing things the better way.

Is it a high-risk activity? What is the consequence times likelihood? Is a lot
at stake? What is the blast radius: only you, or a bunch of other people as
well?

When cooking dinner for many people, I might or might not test an improved
technique based on who I am serving and the consequence of failing to implement
the technique.

Sometimes there are obvious wins that can be implemented right away, like
discovering keyboard shortcuts. There is no consequence if you type it wrong,
and the blast radius is minimal.

Other times, you definitely want to stick to what you know, such as not trying a
foreign technical setup right before a company presentation.

## Artifact

Here too I tend to consider risk and blast radius, but also the time and value
of what I am making. Especially when building software for others in a
collaborative environment.

The value tradeoff is not only user value. It might be value for future me, or
colleagues, having to revisit the artifact. It might be value for less
experienced people who take inspiration from it.

## Short version

For process, I usually bookmark the new technique and start implementing it the
next time I do that activity.

For artifacts, I tend to take pride in noticing sunk cost fallacies and the
slaughtering of darlings that usually follows. This is especially obvious if I
am in an environment where I can branch out, such as Git or Figma. If the better
approach fails, I can always return to my first attempt.

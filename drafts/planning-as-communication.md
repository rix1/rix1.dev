Why would we plan in the first place?

On strategy and clarity: cut everything that is not essential to the mid-term
strategy. This clarity brings focus and energy, which drives better execution.

We are planning for two reasons:

1. To help us think
2. To create momentum

We use that momentum to make the distance between where we are and where we want
to go feel more traversable.

## What are our goals?

Begin with the end in mind. Where do we want to go?

Here is one version of a high-level plan:

1. Launch address search on a partner's site.
2. Land the first sale.
3. Complete the first installation.
4. Send the first automated invoice.

These are all very internally oriented. Let's make them more customer-oriented:

1. Site visitors can search their address and get an automated quote. They can
   leave their details and be called within a reasonable amount of time.
2. A new or existing customer can sign an automated contract without manual
   intervention.
3. The customer gets their system planned and installed. They are now producing
   their own energy.
4. The customer receives their first automated invoice as part of the existing
   invoicing system.

I like the customer-oriented version better. It is more verbose, but it is also
more inspiring.

## Plans need fabric and time

Words are nice, but we need to bring these goals from ink on paper into real
life. Real life consists of fabric and time.

To communicate the fabric of our goals, we need to show how those goals
translate into something tangible. Something you can touch, or at least imagine
yourself touching.

This is where demos come in.

Demos are my favorite part of the product development process. Like a well
cooked dinner, they have the ability to wow people and expand their perspective.
For those of us building, demos are a great way to learn more about the domain
and the complexities we are about to enter.

If you are smart, they also provide an opportunity to address four key risks as
early as possible:

- Feasible
- Usable
- Viable
- Valuable

This is a slight rearrangement of Marty Cagan's usual framing, but in the
context of a demo, this is often the order in which you encounter the risks.

## What can a demo be?

A demo can be crafted in many ways. The most important aspect for me is that it
is visual. It does not have to be interactive.

Reasonable alternatives include:

- A Miro board of the customer journey
- A Figma artboard of the customer journey
- A video walkthrough of a preview branch
- Screenshots with annotations

The point is not the tool. The point is making the plan tangible enough that
people can understand it, react to it, and use it as a reference later.

## Goals on a timeline

After making the goals tangible, the next step is placing them on a timeline.

Some processes have a natural order. It would not make sense to start with
automated invoice support and move backwards if the customer has not yet signed
a contract. The timeline should follow the business process where possible.

Then divide tasks into trivial and non-trivial additions to the codebase.

## Blind spots and dark spots

It is important to pay attention to what is not in the plan.

There are many activities that are not necessary for us to trigger, but that
still need to happen somewhere. These are dark spots. There are unlimited dark
spots, which is fine. The universe is mostly empty.

But we do not want too many blind spots. These are activities that should have
been covered, but were not. Blind spots are usually created when planning.

This is why it is important to sense and respond, which in my book is a fancy
way of saying: have regular check-ins and keep an eye on the data.

As an amateur home cook, I know the importance of maintaining the tools and
techniques I use to cook. This not only makes the food taste better, prepared
faster and with fewer dishes, but it also pushes the boundary of what I am
capable of doing.

As an inexperienced cook, I found it difficult to make dinner for one.
Everything was hard.

But with time and practice you level up. One day you suddenly find yourself
planning and making four-course meals for 20 of your parents' colleagues. You
raised the bar, but you continue practicing. In 2019, I was responsible for
serving 60+ people three meals a day for 10 days at a summer camp for children.
That is 1800 servings in total.

There are a ton of factors at play in the process of mastering a skill.

Today, I want to highlight two things that I believe are paramount in my own
learning processes: flow and maintaining a growth mindset. This post is about
what those mean, along with leverage, and how these concepts relate not only to
sharpening your tools, but becoming an effective engineer.

Growth mindset lays the foundation, and thinking about flow helps guide my
attention.

## How to sharpen your knife at work

Let me start by introducing a set of 52-year-old rules that govern our
attention. The 0.1s, 1s, and 10s response time guidelines for software
applications were first proposed by Miller in 1968:

**100ms:** The limit for users feeling that they are directly manipulating data
or objects in the UI. People get the sense that _they_ are changing the data,
not the computer. The system feels instantaneous.

Kitchen analogy: _You_ are cutting the carrot in half.

**1 second:** About the limit for the user's flow of thought to stay
uninterrupted. The user no longer feels they are manipulating the data directly,
but gets the sense that they are instructing the computer to do some work on
their behalf.

Kitchen analogy: You are instructing someone to cut the carrot in half.

**10 seconds:** The limit for keeping attention on the task. The computer should
give feedback indicating when it will be done.

Kitchen analogy: You are instructing someone to julienne the carrot: "I'll get
back to you in two minutes."

[Source: NNGroup](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/response-times-3-important-limits/)

These rules obviously relate to creating interfaces and delightful user
experiences. But how does this map to sharpening your knife and being effective?
This is where flow and leverage come in.

Our brain is a very busy place. Even when we manage to carve out longer
stretches of uninterrupted time in our
[maker schedule](http://www.paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html), we are still
prone to being distracted. Not by Slack notifications or email, but by
ourselves.

The tighter the feedback loop, the faster you learn and the more likely you are
to build up accurate intuition. Intuition is important because it lets you take
leaping shortcuts in your intellectual and problem-solving thinking.

> Among medical specialties, anaesthesiologists benefit from good feedback,
> because the effects of their actions are likely to be quickly evident. In
> contrast, radiologists obtain little information about the accuracy of the
> diagnoses they make and about the pathologies they fail to detect.
>
> From _Thinking, Fast and Slow_, Daniel Kahneman

Just like we use a SHA to represent an entire book with a single hash, intuition
lets you jump from one complex idea to the next without reading through the
entire book.

So intuition is important, and you build it by ensuring short feedback loops.

## Why should you sharpen your knife?

It is not important because of throughput. It is not about counting up the
seconds so you can say "this week I saved three minutes navigating Cloud".

On the contrary, it is important because we reduce time spent in unfocused
idling. I believe reducing unfocused idling induces flow.

In Csikszentmihalyi's words, flow is:

> A state in which people are so involved in an activity that nothing else seems
> to matter; the experience is so enjoyable that people will continue to do it
> even at great cost, for the sheer sake of doing it.

You have probably experienced this while playing video games or doing creative
work like drawing or painting. However, flow can also be experienced in more
surprising scenarios. Personally, I have completely lost the sense of time while
doing dishes. I usually recognize having experienced flow _after_ I exit the
task. It is sort of like dreaming, where you get the feeling of waking up after
a longer stretch.

Other examples to consider:

- Folding clothes
- Sorting documents
- Spending two weeks internationalizing a product

It is not about how fun or boring a task is. We are all accountable for our own
attitude toward something. Every task can be made interesting. Fundamental to
doing this is having a growth mindset: the realization that you have agency and
control over not only your attitude, but also your skill set.

If you lack the skills, establish subgoals and break apart the challenge.

If there is too little challenge, introduce constraints. Use only one hand. Try
to write without using the letter "e". Try to avoid using the mouse.

Using these two tools, flow and a growth mindset, you can get into the habit of
continually sharpening your knife, honing your skills while expanding your tool
belt.

## Demos

The following are examples of minor hacks I have made for myself. The process
looks roughly like this:

1. Identify slow tasks, or tasks that you frequently do while focused.
2. Improve one small part of the task.
3. Practice until the improved version becomes the default.

Examples:

- Find the value of a Heroku environment variable
- Open a terminal
- Change the focused window in the same application
- Resize windows
- Move a window to another screen and center it
- Change workspace in VS Code
- Select the rest of a word
- Select to the end of a line
- Select the next occurrence, previous occurrence, or skip
- Work with multiple cursors
- Expand and shrink selection

## Epilogue

I want to do extraordinary things. Not only to impress my peers, but more
importantly to impress myself. Did I really do that? Was I really part of making
that a success?

We are not only working to impress our customers. We are working to show
ourselves that this thing is now possible because we did our part.
